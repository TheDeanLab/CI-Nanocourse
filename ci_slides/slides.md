# Lecture 11
## Licenses
---

# Why proper licensing matters
## A repository without a license is not truly open source

- A license tells others what they are allowed to do with your code.
- It affects reuse, redistribution, modification, and contribution.
- It also protects you by setting expectations and limitations clearly.
- On GitHub, publishing code publicly does **not** automatically grant reuse rights.

---

# No license = "all rights reserved"
## Public code is still legally restricted without explicit permission

- If a repository has **no license**, others may read it but cannot safely reuse or redistribute it.
- This creates uncertainty for collaborators, students, and companies.
- It can discourage adoption because nobody wants accidental legal risk.
- A missing license is one of the fastest ways to make a project harder to use.

---

# Permissive vs copyleft
## MIT and GPLv3 make different collaboration trade-offs

- **MIT**: very permissive; easy reuse in academic, personal, and commercial projects.
- **GPLv3**: stronger copyleft; modified/redistributed versions must remain under GPL-compatible terms.
- Permissive licenses maximize adoption; copyleft licenses prioritize keeping derivatives open.
- The “best” license depends on your goals, not on a single universal rule.

---

# Choose a license intentionally
## Technical goals and institutional policy both matter

- Start with your project goals:
  - maximize reuse?
  - require openness in derivatives?
  - align with an existing dependency’s license?
- Then check employer/institution policy before publishing.
- If you are developing software for a university, hospital, company, or grant-funded project, ownership may not be yours alone.
- When in doubt, ask your technology transfer / legal / open-source office before release.

---

# Companies may enforce use of their own preferred licenses
## Account for institutional and employer policy requirements
 
 - UT Southwestern’s open-source software license is here: https://www.utsouthwestern.edu/about-us/administrative-offices/technology-development/agreements/open-source-release-of-software.html
 - If you develop software on a UTSW machine or for a UTSW purpose, verify which license policy applies.
 - If your code includes or derives from GPL-licensed software, confirm compatibility with institutional policy before release.
 - The important lesson: your organization’s rules may override your personal preference.
